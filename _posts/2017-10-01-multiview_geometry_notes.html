Terms: planar (2d), collinearity (a straight line is imaged as a straight line)

Planr geometry: geometric viewpoint vs. algebraic viewpoint (coordinates with certen basis) , algebraic approach is helpful for deriving algorithms and practical computation methods.

Geometric method (e.g., euclid) vs algebraic method (e.g., introduce Descartes coordinates; a conic section is represented by a symmetric matrix). In mathematics, a conic section (or simply conic) is a curve obtained as the intersection of the surface of a cone with a plane. The three types of conic section are the hyperbola, the parabola, and the ellipse. A significant advantage of the algebraic approach to geometry is that results derived in this way may more easily be used to derive algo- rithms and practical computational methods. Computation and algorithms are a major concern in this book, which justifies the use of the algebraic method.

Computation: Geometric entities will by default be represented by column vectors.

Homogenous: origins from the equivalent class of vector \((a,b,c)^T\) that represent the same line.
2D Projective Plane defined by lines: The set of equivalence classes of vectors in \(R^3\) − \((0, 0, 0)^T\) forms the projective space \(P^2\). The notation \((0, 0, 0)^T\) indicates that the vector \((0, 0, 0)^T\), which does not correspond to any line, is excluded.
2D Projective Plane defined by points: One may augment \(IR^2\) by adding points with last coordinate x_3 = 0. The resulting space is the set of all homogeneous 3-D vectors, namely the projective space \(IP^2\).

[G] If vector V and kV is equivalent in an given circumstance (k is any none zeor scalar), we say V is in homogenous representation and call it homogenous vector. A 3-vector representing a line is a homogenous vector; A 3-vector representing points in \(R^2\) is an homogenous vector; A 4-vector representing points in \(R^3\) is an homogenous vector;

[Ref] Homogeous vector actually represetns a point. A vector has no concept on position but a point does.
A vector, note that \([X, Y, Z, o]\) is a matrix: $$ v = [v_1, v_2, v_3]^T = v_1X + v_2Y + v_3Z $$ $$ v = [X, Y, Z, o] \times [v_1, v_2, v_3, 0]^T $$
A point: $$ p - o = [p_1, p_2, p_3] = p_1X + p_2Y + p_3Z $$ $$ p = [X, Y, Z, o] \times [p_1, p_2, p_3, 1]^T $$
We can see that the homogenous coordinate for point can represent translation and this is the reason why it is used to dealing with computer vision algirthms.

Intersection of lines: \(x = l \times l' \)

Line joining points: \(l = x \times x' \)

Cross product: calcuating intersection of lines or line joing points, the coordinates involved in the cross product are all homogenous.

Ideal points and lines at infinite: points in \(R^2\) plus ideal points construct the projective space \(P^2\). $$ (1, 0, 0)\dot(x_1, x_2, 0) = 0 $$

Duality Principle: To any theorem of 2-dimensional projective geometry there corresponds a dual theorem, which may be derived by interchanging the roles of points and lines in the original theorem.
we can understand the dulity from the perspective of inner product which shows l \((a, b, c)\) and p \([x, y, z]^T\) are orthgonal.

Degenerate conic

Conic formual in 2d (X, Y) nomal coordinate: $$ aX^2 + bXY + CY^2 + dX + eY + f = 0 $$
convert into homogenous coordinate (X, Y, Z): $$ aX^2 + bXY + CY^2 + dXZ + eYZ + fZ^2 = 0 $$
It can be again described in matrix form: $$ p^TCp = 0 $$
\(p\) is \([X,Y,Z]^T\) and \(C\) is matrix shown below. $$ C = \begin{pmatrix} a & b/2 & d/2 \\ b/2 & c & e/2 \\ d/2 & e/2 & f \\ \end{pmatrix} $$

Degenerate conic

A line trangent to a conic at point \(p\): \( l = Cp \)

Dual Conic: \( l^TC^*l = 0 \), \(C^*\) is the adjoint matrix of \(C\) and \(C^* = C^{-1}\)
Prove (for full rank case): \(l = Cx\) => \(x = C^{-1}l\) => \( (C^{-1}l)^TC(C^{-1}l) = 0\) => \(l^T(C_{-1})^Tl = 0\) => done
note that \(C\) is symmetric that \((C^{-1})^T = C^{-1}\)

Degenerate Conic: when \(C\) is not full rank.

Projective Transformations: A projectivity is an invertible mapping h from \(IP^2\) to itself such that three points \(x_1\), \(x_2\) and \(x_3\) lie on the same line if and only if \(h(x_1)\), \(h(x_2)\) and \(h(x_3)\) do.
A projectivity is also called a collineation, a projective transformation or a homography: the terms are synonymous.
More on "collineation", the key point of projectivity is the maintenance of linearity after transformation, which means "col"-lineation.
